Overview

The history of computer network architecture is filled with fascinating milestones and trivia. The journey began with ARPANET, where the first message sent on October 29, 1969, was "LO" before the system crashed. This early network utilized packet switching, a concept independently developed by Paul Baran and Donald Davies in the early 1960s. Ethernet, another cornerstone of networking, was conceived by Robert Metcalfe in 1973 at Xerox PARC, famously sketched on a napkin. TCP/IP became the standard protocol for ARPANET on January 1, 1983, a date known as "Flag Day". The World Wide Web, created by Tim Berners-Lee, had its first website go live on August 6, 1991, hosted on a NeXT computer at CERN. Wi-Fi, a term coined as a play on "Hi-Fi", revolutionized wireless networking, though it does not stand for "Wireless Fidelity" as commonly thought. The first domain name, "symbolics.com", was registered on March 15, 1985. Internet usage exploded from 16 million users in 1995 to 361 million by 2000. The IBM Simon Personal Communicator, released in 1994, is considered the first smartphone, offering email and basic web browsing. Lastly, IPv6 was developed to address the limitations of IPv4, providing an almost unimaginably large address space capable of generating 340 undecillion unique IP addresses. These milestones highlight the rapid evolution and key innovations that have shaped modern computer network architecture.
